## Problem 1: Fourier Series

<Problem id="1" title="Fourier Series">
a) For some $N \in \mathbb{N}$, let $x \in l_2\Big( (-N, -(N-1), \cdots, N-1, N); \mathbb{C} \Big)$ with

$$x(n) = 1_{\{|n| \le N_1\}}$$

Find the Fourier series expansion of $x$. Study the case with $N_1 = N$, and the case with $N_1 = 0$.

b) For some $T \in \mathbb{R}_+$, let $x \in L_2([-\frac{T}{2}, \frac{T}{2}]; \mathbb{C})$ with

$$x(t) = 1_{\{|t| \le T_1\}}$$

Find the Fourier series expansion of $x$. Study the cases with (i) $T_1 = \frac{T}{2}$, and (ii) the case where, with $T_1 = \frac{1}{2n}$, we have

$$x_n(t) = n1_{\{|t| \le \frac{1}{2n}\}},$$

as $n \to \infty$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**a)** We have for $k = 0, 1, \cdots, 2N+1$,

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2N+1}\right) = \sum_{n=-N_1}^{N_1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2N+1}} e^{-i\frac{2\pi k}{2N+1}n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2N+1}} e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{2N+1}N_1} \sum_{n=0}^{2N_1} e^{-i\frac{2\pi k}{2N+1}n}$$

For $k \neq 0$, we have

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2N+1}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2N+1}} e^{i\frac{2\pi k N_1}{2N+1}} \frac{(1 - e^{-i\frac{2\pi k(2N_1+1)}{2N+1}})}{1 - e^{-i\frac{2\pi k}{2N+1}}}$$

For $k = 0$, we have

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{0}{2N+1}\right) = \frac{2N_1 + 1}{\sqrt{2N+1}}$$

If $N_1 = N$: For $k \neq 0$

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2N+1}\right) = 0$$

and for $k = 0$, we have

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{0}{2N+1}\right) = \sqrt{2N+1}$$

For the other extreme, if $N_1 = 0$, we have for all $k \in \{0, \cdots, 2N+1\}$

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2N+1}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2N+1}}$$

**b)** We have the expansion (in the $L_2$-sense):

$$x(t) = \sum_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{T}\right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}} e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{T}t}$$

with

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{T}\right) = \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} x(t) \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}} e^{-i\frac{2\pi k}{T}t} dt$$

Thus, with $x$ as given, we have

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{T}\right) = \int_{-T_1}^{T_1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}} e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{T}t} = \frac{e^{i\frac{2\pi k T_1}{T}} - e^{-i\frac{2\pi k T_1}{T}}}{i2\pi k / \sqrt{T}} = \frac{\sin(2\pi k \frac{T_1}{T})}{2\pi k \frac{T_1}{T}} \cdot \frac{2T_1}{\sqrt{T}}$$

If $T_1 = \frac{T}{2}$, we have that $\hat{x}(\frac{k}{T}) = 0$ for all $k \neq 0$ and $\hat{x}(\frac{0}{T}) = \sqrt{T}$.

If $T_1 = \frac{1}{2n}$, with $x_n(t) = n1_{\{|t| \le T_1\}}$, then we observe that $\hat{x}_n(\frac{k}{T}) \to \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$ for all $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

We observe from these examples that, as a general insight (which can be made more rigorous under additional conditions), if we expand the signal in time domain, we shrink it in the frequency domain; and if we shrink it in time domain, we expand it in the frequency domain.
</Solution>

## Problem 2: Fourier Series

<Problem id="2" title="Fourier Series">
In $l_2\Big( \{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\}; \mathbb{C} \Big)$, we observed that the Fourier series

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{N}n}, \quad k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\},$$

provides a complete orthonormal sequence, hence, provides a basis. Let $x \in l_2\Big( \{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\}; \mathbb{C} \Big)$ be given as:

$$x(n) = 2 + 3\cos\left(\frac{4\pi}{N}n\right) + \cos\left(\frac{6\pi}{N}n\right) + \cos\left(\frac{6\pi}{N}n + \frac{\pi}{2}\right),$$

for all $n$.

What is the Fourier series expansion of $x$?
</Problem>

<Solution>
One writes

$$x(n) = 2 + 3\cos\left(\frac{4\pi}{N}n\right) + \cos\left(\frac{6\pi}{N}n\right) + \cos\left(\frac{6\pi}{N}n + \frac{\pi}{2}\right),$$

as

$$x(n) = 2 + \frac{3}{2}(e^{i\frac{4\pi}{N}n} + e^{-i\frac{4\pi}{N}n}) + \frac{1}{2}(e^{i\frac{6\pi}{N}n} + e^{-i\frac{6\pi}{N}n}) + \frac{1}{2}(e^{i(\frac{6\pi}{N}n + \frac{\pi}{2})} + e^{-i(\frac{6\pi}{N}n + \frac{\pi}{2})})$$

And finally,

$$x(n) = 2 + \frac{3}{2}e^{i\frac{2\pi \cdot 2}{N}n} + \frac{3}{2}e^{i\frac{(N-2)2\pi}{N}n} + \frac{1}{2}(1 + e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}})e^{i\frac{2\pi \cdot 3}{N}n} + \frac{1}{2}(1 - e^{-i\frac{\pi}{2}})e^{i\frac{2\pi(N-3)}{N}n}$$

By matching the terms with the Fourier expansion:

$$x(n) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{N}\right) e^{i(2\pi \frac{k}{N})n},$$

we obtain:

$$\hat{x}(0) = 2\sqrt{N}, \quad \hat{x}\left(\frac{2}{N}\right) = \hat{x}\left(\frac{N-2}{N}\right) = \sqrt{N}\frac{3}{2}$$

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{3}{N}\right) = \sqrt{N}\frac{1}{2}(1+i), \quad \hat{x}\left(\frac{N-3}{N}\right) = \sqrt{N}\frac{1}{2}(1-i)$$

where we use $e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}} = i$. The rest of the coefficients: $\hat{x}(\frac{k}{N})$ are all zero for $k \notin \{0, 2, 3, N-3, N-2\}$.
</Solution>

## Problem 3: Fourier Series

<Problem id="3" title="Fourier Series">
In class, we observed that in $L_2([-\frac{P}{2}, \frac{P}{2}]; \mathbb{C})$, the sequence of signals

$$\left\{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{P}} e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t}, k \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}$$

provides a complete orthonormal sequence.

Let $f \in L_2([-\frac{P}{2}, \frac{P}{2}]; \mathbb{C})$ such that $f(-\frac{P}{2}) = f(\frac{P}{2})$. Suppose $f$ is differentiable.

Find the Fourier expansion of $g(t) = f'(t)$ for $t \in [-\frac{P}{2}, \frac{P}{2}]$ in terms of the Fourier series coefficients of $f$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Let $\hat{f}(\frac{k}{\sqrt{P}})$ be the coefficient multiplying $e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t}$ for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ in the expansion. Considering the same for $g$, we obtain from Integration by Parts:

$$\hat{g}\left(\frac{k}{\sqrt{P}}\right) = \int_{-\frac{P}{2}}^{\frac{P}{2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{P}} g(t) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t} dt$$

$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{P}} f(t) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t} \Big|_{-\frac{P}{2}}^{\frac{P}{2}} - \int_{-\frac{P}{2}}^{\frac{P}{2}} (-i2\pi \frac{k}{P}) \frac{1}{\sqrt{P}} f(t) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t} dt$$

$$= (i2\pi \frac{k}{P}) \int_{-\frac{P}{2}}^{\frac{P}{2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{P}} f(t) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{P}t} dt$$

$$= (i2\pi \frac{k}{P}) \hat{f}\left(\frac{k}{\sqrt{P}}\right)$$
</Solution>

## Problem 5: Matlab and Fourier Series

<Problem id="5" title="Matlab and Fourier Series">
a) Let $x \in L_2([0,2]; \mathbb{C})$ be given by:

$$x(t) = 1_{\{t \le 1\}}t + 1_{\{1 \le t \le 2\}}(1 - t)$$

Let $\hat{x}(\frac{k}{2})$ denote the Fourier series coefficient corresponding to $\{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{2}t}, t \in [0,2]\}$.

With Matlab, generate the plot of the signal

$$x_N(t) = \sum_{k=-N}^{N} \hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2}\right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{2}t}, \quad t \in [0,2]$$

for $N = 5, 10$ and $15$. Here $\hat{x}(\frac{k}{P})$ are the Fourier Series expansion coefficients.

Observe that, the signal looks more and more like the original signal as $N$ gets larger.

b) Prove that $\lim_{N \to \infty} \int (x(t) - x_N(t))^2 dt = 0$.

Hint: Use the properties of Hilbert spaces and the fact that $\{\frac{1}{\sqrt{P}}e^{i2\pi \frac{k}{P}}\}$ forms a complete orthonormal sequence. We had proved a related theorem in class in the context of Hilbert spaces. You could invoke this result directly in your argument.

c) Does for a general $x \in L_2([0,2]; \mathbb{C})$,

$$\sup_{t \in [0,2]} |x(t) - x_N(t)| \to 0,$$

as $N \to \infty$? Explain your argument.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**b)** We note here that for a given Hilbert space $H$ and a complete orthonormal sequence $\{e_k, k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ in $H$, if for $x \in H$, we can write $x = \sum_i \alpha_i e_i$, it must be that

$$\lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{i=-N}^{N} \alpha_i e_i = x$$

and, furthermore

$$\lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{i=-N}^{N} |\alpha_i|^2 = (\|x\|_2)^2$$

This was proven in class while studying Hilbert spaces. Since the space $L_2([0,2]; \mathbb{C})$ is a separable Hilbert space and Fourier series is a complete orthonormal sequence, the discussion applies.

**c)** The convergence in $L_2([0,P]; \mathbb{C})$ is, naturally, in the $L_2$ norm (and not necessarily in the supremum norm). Recall the way we proved the result that complex exponentials are complete: we showed first that continuous functions can be approximated uniformly; and then used the fact that continuous functions are a dense family in $L_2$. Fourier series can be used to reconstruct any continuous function supported on a compact set pointwise (and in fact uniformly over compact sets).

However, Fourier series expansions may not converge pointwise for general functions!

Consider the following signal. Let

$$x(t) = 1_{\{0 \le t \le M\}},$$

and $x \in L_2([0, 2\pi]; \mathbb{C})$. The Fourier series expansion of this signal can be obtained by the computation of the following for all $k \in \mathbb{Z}$: For $k \neq 0$:

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{2\pi}\right) = \int_0^{M} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-ikt} dt = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \frac{1}{-ik}[e^{-ikM} - 1] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \frac{1}{ik}[1 - e^{-ikM}]$$

For $k = 0$, we obtain

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{0}{2\pi}\right) = M/\sqrt{2\pi}$$

The expansion is

$$\tilde{x}(t) = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k \in [-N,N] \cap \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}} \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{1}{ik}[1 - e^{-ikM}]e^{ikt} + \frac{M}{2\pi}$$

Let $t = 0$ and $M = \pi$. Then,

$$\tilde{x}(0) = \frac{M}{2\pi} + \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k \in [-N,N] \cap \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}} \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{1}{ik}[1 - e^{-ikM}] = \frac{M}{2\pi} + \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{\sin(k\pi)}{\pi k}$$

which is equal to $1/2$ since the second term is zero.

As such, the reconstruction is not equal to the original signal. For our problem, we can write the function as

$$\left( 1_{\{t \le 1\}}t + 1_{\{1 \le t \le 2\}}(2-t) \right) - 1_{\{1 \le t \le 2\}}$$

The first term is continuous. The second term, however, will lead to discontinuity, and ultimately a reconstruction which will be equal to $-1/2$ at $t = 1$. Therefore, the reconstruction will not be exact at $t = 1$, following the discussion above.
</Solution>

## Problem 8: CCFT is Unitary

<Problem id="8" title="CCFT is Unitary">
Show that CCFT is a unitary transformation from $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$ to itself.

Hint: You may use the construction we discussed in class where the $\mathcal{F}_{CC}$ is first defined on $\mathcal{S}$ and then extended to $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
In class, we proved the following:

**Theorem:** Let $T : M \to Y$ be a linear mapping, $Y$ a Banach space, $M$ a dense linear subspace of a space $X$. Furthermore, let $T$ be bounded in the sense that

$$\|T\| = \sup_{x \in M, x \neq 0} \frac{\|Tx\|}{\|x\|} < \infty.$$

Then, there exists a unique extension of $T$ on $X$ to $Y$, denoted by $\bar{T} : X \to Y$, such that $\bar{T}(x) = T(x)$ for $x \in M$ (that is, $T$ and $\bar{T}$ are in agreement on $M$). Furthermore, $\|T\| = \|\bar{T}\|$.

Now, let $M = \mathcal{S}$ (with the functions taking complex values), $X = Y = L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$, $T = \mathcal{F}_{CC}$ and note by Plancherel's Theorem

$$\langle g, h \rangle = \langle \hat{g}, \hat{h} \rangle, \qquad h, g \in \mathcal{S}$$

that

$$\|\mathcal{F}_{CC}\| = 1.$$

We define $F_{CC}$ on $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$ to be the unique extension of $\mathcal{F}_{CC}$ from $\mathcal{S} \to L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$ to $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C}) \to L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$. Thus, for $h \in L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$,

$$\langle h, h \rangle = \langle \hat{h}, \hat{h} \rangle, \quad \hat{h} = \mathcal{F}_{CC}(h).$$

By the stated (extension) theorem, it follows that $\mathcal{F}_{CC}$ is also unitary on $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$ with the same operator norm, that is:

$$\langle g, h \rangle = \langle \hat{g}, \hat{h} \rangle, \qquad h, g \in L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C}).$$

Let us make this more explicit. Let $g$ and $h$ be in $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$ and $g_n \to g$, and let $h_n \to h$ with $g_n, h_n \in \mathcal{S}$ being Schwartz signals. By Plancherel's identity:

$$\langle g_n, h_n \rangle = \langle \hat{g}_n, \hat{h}_n \rangle$$

Let us take the limit as $n \to \infty$ on both sides. For the left hand side the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality implies that

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \langle g_n, h_n \rangle = \langle g, h \rangle,$$

because (since $\|h_n\|$ is bounded)

$$|\langle g_n, h_n \rangle - \langle g, h \rangle| = |\langle g_n - g, h_n \rangle + \langle g, h_n - h \rangle| \le \|g_n - g\|\|h_n\| + \|g\|\|h_n - h\| \to 0$$

Likewise

$$|\langle \hat{g}_n, \hat{h}_n \rangle - \langle \hat{g}, \hat{h} \rangle| = |\langle \hat{g}_n - \hat{g}, \hat{h}_n \rangle + \langle \hat{g}, \hat{h}_n - \hat{h} \rangle| \le \|\hat{g}_n - \hat{g}\|\|\hat{h}_n\| + \|\hat{g}\|\|\hat{h}_n - \hat{h}\| \to 0$$

Here, the convergence to zero follows from the fact that $\|\hat{g}_n - \hat{g}\| \to 0$ (since $\|g_n - g\| = \|\hat{g}_n - \hat{g}\| \to 0$ by the discussion above) and that $\|\hat{h}_n\|$ is bounded. This generalizes Plancherel's identity for signals in $L_2(\mathbb{R}; \mathbb{C})$.
</Solution>

## Problem 9: The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) Algorithm

<Problem id="9" title="The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) Algorithm">
Describe the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) Algorithm and explain why the computational complexity is reduced when compared with the usual Fourier Transform computations.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Recall that

$$\mathcal{F}_{DD} : l_2(\{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\}; \mathbb{C}) \to l_2(\{\frac{0}{N}, \frac{1}{N}, \ldots, \frac{N-1}{N}\}; \mathbb{C})$$

so that $\hat{x} = \mathcal{F}_{DD}(x)$ with

$$\hat{x}(f) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(n) e^{-i2\pi fn}, \quad f \in \{\frac{0}{N}, \frac{1}{N}, \ldots, \frac{N-1}{N}\}$$

In the above, we see that there are $N$ complex multiplications and $N$ complex additions for each $f$ (and thus there will be $N$ such computations). Thus, the FFT algorithm in the form above has the computational complexity of $N^2$ complex operations (with one complex operation being equivalent to one complex addition and one complex multiplication).

If $N$ is even, we can write

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{N}\right) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(n) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N}n}$$

$$= \sum_{m=0}^{\frac{N}{2}-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(2m) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N}2m} + \sum_{m=0}^{\frac{N}{2}-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(2m+1) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N}(2m+1)}$$

$$= \sum_{m=0}^{\frac{N}{2}-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(2m) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N/2}m} + \left( \sum_{m=0}^{\frac{N}{2}-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} x(2m+1) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N/2}m} \right) e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N}}$$

Define $x_0(m) = x(2m)$ and $x_1(m) = x(2m+1)$, for $m = 0, 1, \cdots, \frac{N}{2} - 1$. Then, the above leads to

$$\hat{x}\left(\frac{k}{N}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \hat{x}_0\left(\frac{k}{\frac{N}{2}}\right) + e^{-i2\pi \frac{k}{N}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \hat{x}_1\left(\frac{k}{\frac{N}{2}}\right)$$

Thus, as we see, the above leads to a parallel processing of two smaller length transforms. If $N$ is a power of 2, we can continue with this approach to a building block of length $N = 2$. By inductively splitting the summations in the expansions as above, the FFT algorithm then reduces the computational complexity for the $\mathcal{F}_{DD}$ from $N^2$ complex operations to $N \log_2(N)$ (with $N$ being a power of 2) such operations.
</Solution>
