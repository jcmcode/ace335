## Problem 1: First-Order ODE System

<Problem id="1" title="First-Order ODE System">
Express the following differential equation as a system of first-order differential equations in matrix form, by defining $x_1 = y$, $x_2 = y^{(1)}$ and $x_3 = y^{(2)}$:

$$y^{(3)} + 5y^{(2)} + 5y^{(1)} - 4y = 0.$$
</Problem>

<Solution>
Define $x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}$. It follows that

$$\begin{bmatrix} x_1' \\ x_2' \\ x_3' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 4 & -5 & -5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}$$
</Solution>

## Problem 2: Jordan Form

<Problem id="2" title="Jordan Form">
Find the Jordan form for the following matrices:

a)
$$A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -6 \\ 4 & -5 \end{bmatrix}$$

b)
$$A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Hint: You will have to find a generalized eigenvector for part b.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Part a)** For part a, the solutions to $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ leads to $\lambda^2 - 1 = 0$ and hence the eigenvalues are 1 and -1. As such, both eigenvalues have algebraic multiplicity equal to 1. The geometric multiplicity is also equal to one for both multiplicities. Eigenvectors can be computed as $[3 \quad 2]^T$ and $[1 \quad 1]^T$.

One then obtains:

$$J_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

**Part b)** For part b, we obtain two eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 2$ and $\lambda_2 = -1$. The second eigenvalue $-1$ is an eigenvalue with algebraic multiplicity 2. We obtain the eigenvector for $\lambda_1$ as

$$v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},$$

and an eigenvector for $\lambda_2$ as

$$v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

There is no other eigenvector for $\lambda_2$ (hence, the geometric multiplicity for $-1$ is one, and the null-space for $(A - \lambda_2 I)$ is one-dimensional). Hence, one needs to find a generalized eigenvector for being able to find a matrix $P$ to apply a similarity transformation. We could find this eigenvector by writing:

$$(A - \lambda_2)v_3 = v_2,$$

which leads to

$$v_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Hence, by applying a similarity transformation with matrix $P = [v_1 \quad v_2 \quad v_3]$, we obtain the Jordan form

$$J_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix},$$
</Solution>

## Problem 3: Matrix Exponential Commutativity

<Problem id="3" title="Matrix Exponential Commutativity">
Show that for square matrices $A$ and $B$, which commute, that is

$$AB = BA,$$

it follows that

$$e^{(A+B)} = e^A e^B.$$

Hint: Recall that $e^A = \lim_{T\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{T} \frac{A^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k}{k!}$. Complete the following:

$$e^A e^B = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k}{k!} \frac{B^l}{l!}$$

Also, show and use the fact that $(A + B)^k = \sum_{m=0}^{k} \binom{k}{m} A^m B^{k-m}$, when $AB = BA$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Recall that

$$e^A = \lim_{T\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{T} \frac{A^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k}{k!},$$

with the definition that $A^0 = I$. It follows that

$$e^A e^B = \left(\lim_{T\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{T} \frac{A^k}{k!}\right)\left(\lim_{T\to\infty} \sum_{l=0}^{T} \frac{B^l}{l!}\right) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k}{k!} \frac{B^l}{l!}$$

$$= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \sum_{u=k}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!(u-k)!} A^k B^{u-k}$$

$$= \sum_{u=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{u} \frac{1}{k!(u-k)!} A^k B^{u-k}$$

$$= \sum_{u=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{u!} \sum_{k=0}^{u} \frac{u!}{k!(u-k)!} A^k B^{u-k}$$

$$= \sum_{u=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{u!} \sum_{k=0}^{u} \binom{u}{k} A^k B^{u-k}$$

$$= \sum_{u=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{u!} (A + B)^u$$

$$= e^{(A+B)}$$

In the above, (2) follows by defining $u = k + l$, and (3) follows from re-expressing the summation. Step (4) follows from $AB = BA$ and the following:

$$(A + B)^k = \sum_{m=0}^{k} \binom{k}{m} A^m B^{k-m}.$$

We now prove this last statement. Clearly for $k = 1$,

$$(A + B)^1 = \binom{1}{0} B + \binom{1}{1} A$$

Let us proceed by induction. Suppose this is true for $k$. Let us show that it is true for $k + 1$.

$$(A + B)^k(A + B) = \sum_{m=0}^{k} \binom{k}{m} A^{m+1} B^{k-m} + \sum_{m=0}^{k} \binom{k}{m} A^m B^{k+1-m}.$$

Let us separate out the terms involving $A^p B^{k+1-p}$ for $0 \le p \le k + 1$. It turns out that we obtain:

$$\sum_{p=0}^{k+1} A^p B^{k+1-p} \left(\binom{k}{p} + \binom{k}{p-1}\right)$$

Now,

$$\binom{k}{p} + \binom{k}{p-1} = \frac{k!}{p!(k-p)!} + \frac{k!}{(p-1)!(k+1-p)!}$$

$$= \frac{k!}{(p-1)!(k-p)!}\left(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{(k+1-p)}\right)$$

$$= \frac{k!}{(p-1)!(k-p)!}\left(\frac{k+1}{p(k+1-p)}\right)$$

$$= \frac{(k+1)!}{(p)!(k+1-p)!} = \binom{k+1}{p}$$

This completes the proof.
</Solution>

## Problem 4: Derivative of Matrix Exponential

<Problem id="4" title="Derivative of Matrix Exponential">
Let $A$ be a square matrix. Show that $\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = Ae^{At}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Let $t, h$ be scalars. Since $At$ and $Ah$ commute so that $(At)(Ah) = (Ah)(At)$ we have that $e^{A(t+h)} = e^{At}e^{Ah}$. Then,

$$\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{e^{A(t+h)} - e^{At}}{h}$$

$$= e^{At} \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{e^{Ah} - I}{h}$$

$$= e^{At} \lim_{h\to 0} \left(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{A^k h^k}{h(k!)}\right)$$

$$= e^{At} \lim_{h\to 0} \left(A + h\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{A^k h^{k-2}}{k!}\right)$$

$$= Ae^{At}$$

The final line follows from the fact that the sum converges to zero as $h \to 0$: let $\bar{A}(i,j) = |A(i,j)|$ be the matrix consisting of the absolute value of the entries of $A$, then for each entry $(i,j)$ of the matrix

$$\left|\left(h\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{A^k h^{k-2}}{k!}\right)(i,j)\right| \le |h|\left(\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{\bar{A}^k |h|^{k-2}}{k!}\right)(i,j) \le |h|\left(\bar{A}^2 \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{\bar{A}^{k-2}|h|^{k-2}}{(k-2)!}\right)(i,j)$$

We have that for an arbitrary $\epsilon > 0$, the following analysis applies:

$$\lim_{h\to 0} |h|\left(\bar{A}^2 \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{\bar{A}^{k-2}|h|^{k-2}}{(k-2)!}\right)(i,j) \le \lim_{h\to 0} |h|\left(\bar{A}^2 \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{\bar{A}^{k-2}|\epsilon|^{k-2}}{k!}\right)(i,j)$$

$$= \lim_{h\to 0} |h|\left(\bar{A}^2 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\bar{A}^k |\epsilon|^k}{k!}\right)(i,j) = \lim_{h\to 0} |h|\bar{A}^2 e^{\bar{A}\epsilon}(i,j) = 0$$

The result follows.
</Solution>

## Problem 5: Computing Matrix Exponential

<Problem id="5" title="Computing Matrix Exponential">
Compute $e^{A_1 t}$, where $A_1$ is defined as in Problem 2.

Hint: The eigenvalues are 1 and -1. The corresponding eigenvectors are $[3 \quad 2]^T$ and $[1 \quad 1]^T$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
From above, with

$$J_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

we can write $J_1 = P^{-1}A_1 P$ where $P$ contains the eigenvectors of $A_1$ in its columns:

$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Since we have,

$$e^{A_1 t} = P e^{\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\right)t} P^{-1},$$

it follows that

$$e^{A_1 t} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} e^t & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-t} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -2 & 3 \end{bmatrix},$$

Hence, we conclude that

$$e^{A_1 t} = \begin{bmatrix} 3e^t - 2e^{-t} & -3e^t + 3e^{-t} \\ 2e^t - 2e^{-t} & -2e^t + 3e^{-t} \end{bmatrix}$$
</Solution>

## Problem 6: Solving a Differential Equation

<Problem id="6" title="Solving a Differential Equation">
Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{5} & \frac{2}{5} \\ \frac{2}{5} & \frac{4}{5} \end{bmatrix}.$$

It can be shown that $A$ has its eigenvalues as 1 and 0 and the eigenvectors are $[1 \quad 2]^T$ and $[-2 \quad 1]^T$.

Now, with $A$ as given, solve the following differential equation:

$$x' = Ax + \begin{bmatrix} e^t \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},$$

with the initial condition $x(0) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
First, we compute $e^{At}$:

$$e^{At} = P e^{\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\right)t} P^{-1}$$

with

$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

leading to

$$e^{At} = \begin{bmatrix} (e^t + 4)/5 & (2e^t - 2)/5 \\ (2e^t - 2)/5 & (4e^t + 1)/5 \end{bmatrix}$$

Following the variation of parameters result we derived in class:

$$x(t) = e^{At}x_0 + \int_{\tau=0}^{t} e^{A(t-\tau)} \begin{bmatrix} e^\tau \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} d\tau,$$

it follows that

$$x(t) = \begin{bmatrix} (e^t + 4)/5 & (2e^t - 2)/5 \\ (2e^t - 2)/5 & (4e^t + 1)/5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \int_{\tau=0}^{t} \begin{bmatrix} (e^\tau e^{t-\tau} + 4)/5 \\ e^\tau(2e^{t-\tau} - 2)/5 \end{bmatrix} d\tau$$

and

$$x(t) = e^t \begin{bmatrix} (1 + \frac{t}{5}) \\ \frac{2t}{5} \end{bmatrix}$$
</Solution>

## Problem 7: Matrix Exponential and Direction Fields

<Problem id="7" title="Matrix Exponential and Direction Fields">
Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{bmatrix}.$$

Compute $e^{At}$.

Consider now an equation of the form $x' = Ax$ (with an arbitrary initial condition). Draw the direction field qualitatively for each of the following cases: $a > 0$, $a < 0$ and $a = 0$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Let

$$E = \begin{bmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & a \end{bmatrix}, \qquad F = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & b \\ -b & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

By writing $A = E + F$ and noting that the matrices on the right hand side commute, that is $EF = FE$, it follows that $e^{At} = e^{Et}e^{Ft}$. We have

$$e^{Ft} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(bt) & \sin(bt) \\ -\sin(bt) & \cos(bt) \end{bmatrix},$$

and since $e^{Et}$ is diagonal, we have that

$$e^{At} = e^{at} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(bt) & \sin(bt) \\ -\sin(bt) & \cos(bt) \end{bmatrix}$$

Consider an initial condition $x(0) \in \mathbb{R}^2$. We can write

$$x(0) = r \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix},$$

where $r = \|x(0)\|_2$ is the magnitude of the initial state and $\theta$ is the angle for such a polar representation. Now, $x(t) = e^{At}x(0)$ writes as

$$x(t) = re^{at} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(bt)\cos(\theta) + \sin(bt)\sin(\theta) \\ -\sin(bt)\cos(\theta) + \cos(bt)\sin(\theta) \end{bmatrix}$$

or using the identities $\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$ and $\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\beta)\cos(\alpha)$, we obtain

$$x(t) = re^{at} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(-bt + \theta) \\ \sin(-bt + \theta) \end{bmatrix}$$

Hence, the solution converges (spirals) to zero if $a < 0$, and spirals to infinity if $a > 0$. If $a = 0$, we have a circular motion.
</Solution>

## Problem 8: System Stabilization through Control

<Problem id="8" title="System Stabilization through Control">
Consider

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = ay(t) + u(t) + r(t)$$

where $a \in \mathbb{R}$ is a scalar, $u(t)$ is the control input and $r(t)$ is some disturbance/noise acting on the system. The disturbance is external, that is, the controller has nothing to do with it.

If $a > 0$, then in the absence of control, the solution to the system is given with

$$y(t) = e^{at}y(0) + \int_0^t e^{a(t-s)} r(s) ds$$

In particular, if $r(s) = M$ for $s \ge 0$ for some constant $M > 0$, with $y(0) = 0$, we have that $\lim_{t\to\infty} |y(t)| = \infty$.

On the other hand, if we apply the control input, using the feedback from the state of the system, by the relation $u(t) = -Ky(t)$, this leads to

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = (a - K)y(t) + r(t)$$

For what values of $K$ can we guarantee that the output, $y(t)$, will remain bounded for every bounded signal $r(t)$? (By bounded we mean that $\|r\|_\infty = \sup_{t \in \mathbb{R}_+} |r(t)| < \infty$).
</Problem>

<Solution>
The output, given the control input $u(t) = -Ky(t)$, writes as

$$y(t) = e^{(a-K)t}y(0) + \int_0^t e^{(a-K)(t-s)} r(s) ds$$

Now, if $\|r\|_\infty > \infty$, we have that, if $a - K < 0$, we have that (with $y(0) = 0$)

$$|y(t)| \le \int_0^t e^{(a-K)(t-s)} \|r\|_\infty ds \le \|r\|_\infty \int_0^t e^{(a-K)(t-s)} ds = \|r\|_\infty \frac{1}{a-K}\left(e^{(a-K)t} - 1\right)$$

This expression is uniformly bounded (over all $t \ge 0$).

On the other hand, if $a - K > 0$, then, with applying $r(s) = M$ for every $s \ge 0$, we have that

$$|y(t)| = \int_0^t e^{(a-K)(t-s)} |M| ds = \frac{1}{a-K}\left(e^{(a-K)t} - 1\right)|M| \to \infty,$$

as $t \to \infty$. Finally, if $a - K = 0$, we have that

$$|y(t)| = \left|\int_0^t M\,ds\right| = |M|t \to \infty$$

as $t \to \infty$.

Thus, the condition for the output to remain bounded for every bounded input is that $a - K < 0$.

Note: If $a$ or $K$ were complex valued, we would require the real part of the difference to be negative.

We thus see that control can be used to shape the input output relation in the system.
</Solution>

## Problem 9: Inverted Pendulum

<Problem id="9" title="Inverted Pendulum">
A very useful case study for understanding non-linear systems is the inverted pendulum on a cart system with masses of the pendulum and cart given with $m$ and $M$, respectively.

The dynamics of the inverted pendulum can be expressed with the following:

$$u = M\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + m\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + ml\cos(\theta)\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} - ml\left(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\right)^2\sin(\theta)$$

$$ml^2\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = mg\sin(\theta)l - m\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}\cos(\theta)l$$

Around the point $(\theta = 0, \frac{d\theta}{dt} = 0)$, we apply the linear approximations $\sin(\theta) \approx \theta$ and $\cos(\theta) \approx 1$, and $\left(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\right)^2 \approx 0$ to arrive at the linearized model. Write this in state space form with $x_1 = y, x_2 = \frac{dy}{dt}, x_3 = \theta, x_4 = \frac{d\theta}{dt}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
After linearization:

$$M\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = u - (m\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + ml\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2})$$

$$l\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = g\theta - \frac{d^2y}{dt^2}$$

Finally, writing $x_1 = y, x_2 = \frac{dy}{dt}, x_3 = \theta, x_4 = \frac{d\theta}{dt}$, and manipulating the equations, we arrive at the linear model in state space form

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{-mg}{M} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{(M+m)g}{Ml} & 0 \end{bmatrix} x + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{M} \\ 0 \\ \frac{-1}{Ml} \end{bmatrix} u,$$

where $x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix}$.

Using some systems theory and control theory, it can be shown that linearization will let us construct a control function/policy/law that makes the idealized linear system stable which in turn makes the original non-linear system locally stable around the (open-loop unstable) equilibrium point.
</Solution>
