## Problem 1: Definition of Differentiability (Fill in the Blanks)

<Problem title="Definition of Differentiability (Fill in the Blanks)">
Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ be open, and let $\vec{p}_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0) \in U$. Let $\vec{F} : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be a function. Let $F_1, F_2$ denote the components of $\vec{F}$. Suppose that $\vec{F}$ is differentiable at $\vec{p}_0$.

Fill in the blanks by choosing from the options provided.

- The derivative of $\vec{F}$ at $\vec{p}_0$ is a linear function from $\boxed{(a)}$ to $\boxed{(b)}$.
- For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\boxed{(c)} \leq \varepsilon \boxed{(d)}$ provided $\boxed{(e)}$.
- For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\boxed{(f)}$ provided $\boxed{(g)}$.
- The derivative of $F_2$ at $\vec{p}_0$ is the linear function $T$ from $\boxed{(h)}$ to $\boxed{(i)}$ given by $T\vec{v} = \boxed{(j)}$.

Options:
1. $\mathbb{R}$  2. $\mathbb{R}^2$  3. $\mathbb{R}^3$  4. $U$  5. $\mathbb{R}^5$  6. $\partial U$
7. $\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\|$  8. $\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \varepsilon$  9. $\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \delta$
10. $\|\vec{h}\|$  11. $\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0) - D\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)(\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0)\|$
12. $\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0 + \vec{h}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)\|$  13. $\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)\| < \varepsilon$
14. $\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)\| < \delta$  15. $D\vec{F}$  16. $\nabla F_2(\vec{p}_0) \cdot \vec{v}$
17. $(\nabla F_2(\vec{p}_0))^\top \vec{v}$  18. $\lim_{\vec{p} \to \vec{p}_0} \vec{F}(\vec{p})$ exists  19. $DF_2(\vec{v})$
20. $\sum_{i=1}^{2} \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial y}(\vec{p}_0) v_i$, where $\vec{v} = (v_1, v_2)$
</Problem>

<Solution>
Recall the definition of differentiability: $\vec{F}$ is differentiable at $\vec{p}_0$ if there is a linear map $D\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0) : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ such that
$$\lim_{\vec{p} \to \vec{p}_0} \frac{\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0) - D\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)(\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0)\|}{\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\|} = 0.$$

This is the statement: $\forall \varepsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that
$$\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0) - D\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)(\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0)\| \leq \varepsilon \|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| \quad \text{whenever } \|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \delta.$$

Also, differentiability implies continuity: $\forall \varepsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)\| < \varepsilon$ whenever $\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \delta$.

**Answers:**

- (a) **3** ($\mathbb{R}^3$) — domain of derivative
- (b) **2** ($\mathbb{R}^2$) — codomain of derivative
- (c) **11** ($\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0) - D\vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)(\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0)\|$)
- (d) **7** ($\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\|$)
- (e) **9** ($\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \delta$)
- (f) **13** ($\|\vec{F}(\vec{p}) - \vec{F}(\vec{p}_0)\| < \varepsilon$) — continuity
- (g) **9** ($\|\vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\| < \delta$)
- (h) **3** ($\mathbb{R}^3$) — $F_2$ is a scalar function on $\mathbb{R}^3$, its derivative maps $\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$
- (i) **1** ($\mathbb{R}$)
- (j) **16** ($\nabla F_2(\vec{p}_0) \cdot \vec{v}$) — the derivative of a scalar function is the gradient dot product
</Solution>

## Problem 2: Implicit Function Theorem

<Problem title="Implicit Function Theorem">
Let $F_1, F_2 : \mathbb{R}^4 \to \mathbb{R}$ be two $C^1$ functions and consider the system
$$(\star) \quad \begin{cases} F_1(x, y, u, v) = 0, \\ F_2(x, y, u, v) = 0. \end{cases}$$
Suppose that $F_1(1, 0, 0, 0) = F_2(1, 0, 0, 0) = 0$ and
$$\tfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial x}(1,0,0,0) = 2, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial u}(1,0,0,0) = 0, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_2}{\partial x}(1,0,0,0) = 2, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_2}{\partial u}(1,0,0,0) = -1$$
$$\tfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}(1,0,0,0) = -1, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial v}(1,0,0,0) = -1, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_2}{\partial y}(1,0,0,0) = 0, \quad \tfrac{\partial F_2}{\partial v}(1,0,0,0) = -1.$$

(a) Explain why, in a neighbourhood of the point $(1, 0, 0, 0)$, $(\star)$ defines $u$ and $v$ as functions of $x$ and $y$, say $u = g_1(x, y)$ and $v = g_2(x, y)$.

(b) Find $g_1(1, 0)$ and $g_2(1, 0)$.

(c) Explain why $\dfrac{\partial g_1}{\partial x}(1, 0)$ and $\dfrac{\partial g_2}{\partial x}(1, 0)$ exist and then compute them.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Part (a):** By the Implicit Function Theorem, if $F_1, F_2 \in C^1$, $F_1(\vec{p}) = F_2(\vec{p}) = 0$ at $\vec{p} = (1, 0, 0, 0)$, and the Jacobian of $(F_1, F_2)$ with respect to $(u, v)$ at $\vec{p}$ is invertible, then $(\star)$ locally defines $u, v$ as $C^1$ functions of $x, y$.

Compute the Jacobian:
$$J = \begin{pmatrix} \partial F_1/\partial u & \partial F_1/\partial v \\ \partial F_2/\partial u & \partial F_2/\partial v \end{pmatrix}(1,0,0,0) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$
$\det J = 0 \cdot (-1) - (-1)(-1) = -1 \neq 0$. So $J$ is invertible, and IFT applies.

**Part (b):** By construction, $g_1(1, 0) = u_0 = 0$ and $g_2(1, 0) = v_0 = 0$.

**Part (c):** Since $g_1, g_2$ are $C^1$ (by IFT), their partial derivatives exist. To compute them, differentiate the identities $F_i(x, y, g_1(x,y), g_2(x,y)) = 0$ with respect to $x$:
$$\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial u} \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial v} \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x} = 0, \quad i = 1, 2.$$

At $(1, 0, 0, 0)$:

$i = 1$: $2 + 0 \cdot \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x} + (-1) \cdot \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x} = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x}(1, 0) = 2$.

$i = 2$: $2 + (-1) \cdot \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x} + (-1) \cdot \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x} = 0 \Rightarrow 2 - \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x} - 2 = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x}(1, 0) = 0$.

So $\dfrac{\partial g_1}{\partial x}(1, 0) = 0$ and $\dfrac{\partial g_2}{\partial x}(1, 0) = 2$.
</Solution>

## Problem 3: True/False Statements on Continuity and Differentiability

<Problem title="True/False Statements on Continuity and Differentiability">
For each statement, say whether it is true or false. Justify your answers.

(a) If $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then $f([0, 1])$ is closed.

(b) $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ is a $G_\delta$ set.

(c) If $F : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at $\vec{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, then all first-order partial derivatives of $F$ at $\vec{a}$ exist.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Part (a):** **TRUE.** $[0, 1]$ is compact in $\mathbb{R}$. The continuous image of a compact set is compact: $f([0, 1])$ is compact in $\mathbb{R}$. Compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (with Euclidean topology) are closed and bounded (Heine–Borel), hence closed.

**Part (b):** **FALSE.** $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ is a $G_\delta$ set. Enumerate $\mathbb{Q} = \{q_1, q_2, \ldots\}$. Then
$$\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q} = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty (\mathbb{R} \setminus \{q_n\}),$$
and each $\mathbb{R} \setminus \{q_n\}$ is open. So $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ *is* a $G_\delta$ set. The statement is **TRUE**.

**Part (c):** **TRUE.** If $F$ is differentiable at $\vec{a}$, then for each standard basis vector $\vec{e}_i$,
$$\lim_{t \to 0} \frac{F(\vec{a} + t\vec{e}_i) - F(\vec{a})}{t} = DF(\vec{a})\vec{e}_i$$
exists, which is exactly $\dfrac{\partial F}{\partial x_i}(\vec{a})$.

To confirm: differentiability means $F(\vec{a} + \vec{h}) - F(\vec{a}) - DF(\vec{a})\vec{h} = o(\|\vec{h}\|)$. Taking $\vec{h} = t\vec{e}_i$:
$$\frac{F(\vec{a} + t\vec{e}_i) - F(\vec{a})}{t} = \frac{DF(\vec{a})(t\vec{e}_i)}{t} + \frac{o(|t|)}{t} = DF(\vec{a})\vec{e}_i + o(1) \to DF(\vec{a})\vec{e}_i$$
as $t \to 0$. So $\partial F/\partial x_i(\vec{a}) = DF(\vec{a})\vec{e}_i$ exists.
</Solution>

## Problem 4: Clairaut, Chain Rule

<Problem title="Clairaut, Chain Rule">
(a) Explain why there exists no $C^2$ function $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ such that
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x, y) = e^y \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x, y) = e^x.$$

(b) Assume that $\varphi : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a differentiable function such that $\varphi'(0) = 10$. Let $h(s, t) = \varphi(3s - 4t)$. Explicitly write the derivative of $h$ at the point $(4, 3)$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Part (a):** If such $f \in C^2$ exists, then by Clairaut's (Schwarz's) theorem, mixed partials are equal:
$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}.$$

Compute:
$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^y) = e^y.$$
$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^x) = e^x.$$

But $e^y = e^x$ does not hold for all $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ (e.g., at $(0, 1)$: $e \neq 1$). Contradiction, so no such $C^2$ function exists.

**Part (b):** Let $u = 3s - 4t$. Then $h(s, t) = \varphi(u)$. By the chain rule:
$$\frac{\partial h}{\partial s} = \varphi'(u) \cdot 3 = 3\varphi'(3s - 4t),$$
$$\frac{\partial h}{\partial t} = \varphi'(u) \cdot (-4) = -4\varphi'(3s - 4t).$$

At $(s, t) = (4, 3)$: $u = 3(4) - 4(3) = 12 - 12 = 0$, so $\varphi'(u) = \varphi'(0) = 10$.

$$\frac{\partial h}{\partial s}(4, 3) = 3 \cdot 10 = 30, \quad \frac{\partial h}{\partial t}(4, 3) = -4 \cdot 10 = -40.$$

The derivative of $h$ at $(4, 3)$ is the linear map $Dh(4, 3) : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ given by the gradient:
$$Dh(4, 3) = \nabla h(4, 3) = (30, -40),$$
so $Dh(4, 3)(v_1, v_2) = 30 v_1 - 40 v_2$.
</Solution>
